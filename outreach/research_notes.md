# PUC Rate Case Tracker — Outreach Research Notes

## Recent Major Rate Cases Making News (2025-2026)

### PG&E (California)
PG&E submitted its 2027-2030 General Rate Case filing to the CPUC on May 15, 2025. While PG&E has actually been cutting residential electric rates (down 11% from January 2024 to January 2026), they are implementing a new Base Services Charge starting March 2026, which adds a fixed monthly charge while lowering per-kWh prices. The California Public Advocates Office characterized the GRC filing as "another multi-billion dollar rate increase." PG&E rate design is one of the most closely watched regulatory proceedings in the country.

### Duke Energy (Multi-State)
Duke Energy is active in rate cases across multiple states simultaneously, making it a perfect case study for the tracker:
- **North Carolina (DEC + DEP):** Decision expected late 2026, new rates January 2027. Public hearings scheduled for spring 2026.
- **South Carolina (DEC):** Decision expected early 2026, new rates March 2026. Typical residential increase of $10.38/month.
- **Indiana:** OUCC approved approximately $295.7M in annual revenue increases — significantly less than Duke's $491.5M request (40% reduction). This request-vs-approved gap is exactly the kind of analysis the tracker enables.

### Rocky Mountain Power (Utah)
Sierra Club helped block Rocky Mountain Power's attempt to raise bills by more than 18% in 2025, protecting families from costs tied to fossil-fuel investments. This case illustrates the consumer advocacy angle.

### AEP (West Virginia)
After organizing from Sierra Club and advocacy groups, the West Virginia PUC rejected most of AEP's rate increase request in 2025, cutting costs for ratepayers. Thousands of public comments were submitted.

### Eversource / Con Edison (New York)
AARP New York has been especially vocal on utility rate reform, calling for full review of rate case expenses. 1.2 million NYS households are 60+ days behind on utility bills, totaling nearly $1.9B in arrears. Energy affordability is at crisis levels.

## Academic Papers and Research on Utility Regulation

### Performance-Based Ratemaking (PBR)
Performance-based regulation is a major trend in utility ratemaking. NARUC hosted a dedicated conference on PBR in 2025. Key approaches include:
- Revenue decoupling (relaxing the link between revenue and grid use)
- Performance metrics and incentive mechanisms
- Targeted incentives for underused practices
- Multi-year rate plans (MRPs)

Washington State UTC is actively implementing PBR frameworks, and Hawaii, Illinois, and New York are case studies from the PBR State Working Group (2024).

A notable academic debate exists around whether multi-year rate plans actually improve outcomes vs. traditional ratemaking. ScienceDirect published "Multi-year rate plans are better than traditional ratemaking: Not so fast" (2023), arguing that poorly structured MRPs can perform worse.

### Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory
LBL published "Electricity Rate Designs for Large Loads: Evolving Practices and Opportunities" (January 2025), examining how rate designs need to adapt for data centers, EV charging, and other large loads. Also published a comprehensive study on "State Performance-Based Regulation Using Multiyear Rate Plans for U.S. Electric Utilities."

### Key Academic Researchers
- **Severin Borenstein (UC Berkeley):** Electricity markets, utility pricing, distributional effects of energy policy. Co-authored with Lucas Davis on utility rate topics.
- **Lucas Davis (UC Berkeley):** Electricity and natural gas regulation, pricing in competitive and non-competitive markets.
- **Karen Palmer (RFF):** Economics of environmental, climate, and public utility regulation of the electric power sector.
- **Jenya Kahn-Lang (RFF):** Utility regulation, retail electricity markets, rate design, distributed energy resources.
- **Christopher Knittel (MIT):** Environmental economics, industrial organization, energy policy.
- **Paul Joskow (MIT):** Pioneered research on electricity sector liberalization with "Markets for Power."

## The Data Gap in Rate Case Tracking

### What Exists Today

**S&P Global Regulatory Research Associates (RRA):**
- The only comprehensive cross-state rate case database
- Covers all "major" rate cases (>$5M utility request or >$3M commission approval)
- 30+ year archive dating back to 1980
- Available on S&P Capital IQ Pro platform
- **Cost:** Thousands of dollars per year — inaccessible to researchers, consumer advocates, journalists, and small consulting firms
- RRA is the de facto industry standard, cited in PUC proceedings and regulatory filings

**Individual State PUC Docket Systems:**
- Each state has its own search interface, filing format, and data structure
- Some are searchable (California CPUC), others are barely functional
- No standardized data fields across states
- No API access in most cases
- Requires manual navigation and PDF extraction

**NARUC:**
- Publishes occasional summary reports on rate case trends
- Does not provide structured, downloadable data
- Focuses on aggregate trends rather than case-level detail

### What Does NOT Exist (The Gap We Fill)

There is no **free, structured, cross-state database** of utility rate case decisions that:
1. Normalizes data across state PUC systems into a consistent schema
2. Includes both filing details (utility request) and decision details (commission approval)
3. Provides downloadable exports (CSV, JSON, Excel)
4. Offers an interactive dashboard for exploration and visualization
5. Is accessible to researchers, advocates, journalists, and small firms without a paid subscription

This is a genuine data gap. Consumer advocacy organizations (AARP, NCLC, Sierra Club) spend significant resources manually tracking rate cases across states. Academic researchers are limited in their ability to do cross-state comparative studies. Journalists covering rate cases have to contact each PUC individually.

## Competitors and Differentiation

### S&P Global Regulatory Research Associates (RRA)
- **Strength:** 40-year track record, comprehensive coverage, industry standard
- **Weakness:** Extremely expensive, locked behind S&P Capital IQ Pro paywall, not designed for public interest use
- **Our differentiation:** Free/open, structured for download, interactive dashboard, accessible to non-corporate users

### Gridium
- **Focus:** Building energy data analytics, not rate case tracking
- **Not a direct competitor** — Gridium focuses on meter data and energy management, not regulatory proceedings

### Halcyon
- **Focus:** AI-powered structuring of regulatory filings from FERC and DOE
- **Potential competitor** if they expand to state PUC filings
- **Current gap:** Focused on federal-level filings, not state PUC dockets
- **Our differentiation:** Already have state-level data; rule-based extraction (no LLM dependency)

### Arcadia
- **Focus:** Utility data platform (tariff sheets, bills, market pricing)
- **Not a direct competitor** — Arcadia focuses on consumer-facing utility data, not regulatory proceedings

### FERC eLibrary / FERC.gov
- **Focus:** Federal-level proceedings only
- **Not a direct competitor** — our tracker covers state PUC proceedings

### Individual State PUC Websites
- **Strength:** Authoritative source data
- **Weakness:** No cross-state normalization, no structured exports, inconsistent interfaces
- **Our differentiation:** We aggregate and normalize data from these sources into a single, consistent database
